# Generic Drift — A Board Workbook

*A companion exercise to* **Generic Drift** *(Volume 2 of the Carlorbiz Drift Trilogy).*

*Designed to be worked through by a board, on its own organisation, in roughly two and a half hours.*

*Version 2.0 — adds Part 2 (Score and Act) drawn from Chapter 10's diagnostic dimensions, alongside the original Part 1 (Detect) drawn from Chapter 6's eight warning signs.*

---

## Before you begin

This workbook is a bonus for boards. It is not the book. It assumes you have read *Generic Drift*, or at minimum that the directors filling it in have. If your Chair or CEO has just handed you a copy of the book and asked you to read it before the next board meeting, this workbook is the agenda for that meeting.

You will need three things at the meeting:

- A printed copy of this workbook for each director
- A small set of recent primary documents from your organisation: the last five years of strategic plans, annual reports, CEO letters or chair statements, and one or two recent board papers. Have them physically in the room, or accessible on a single shared screen
- Two and a half hours of board time. The workbook takes about two and a quarter hours to work through; the conversation it produces takes another fifteen to thirty

The workbook has two parts. Each does a different kind of work, and a board genuinely benefits from doing both.

**Part 1 — Detect.** Eight signal worksheets, one per warning sign named in Chapter 6 of the book. The question is: *is drift happening, and where can we see it?* This part is evidence-based. You will be asked to find and cite specific passages in your own primary documents.

**Part 2 — Score and Act.** Eight diagnostic dimensions drawn from Chapter 10. The question is: *given what we have just seen, where should we act first?* This part is operational and prioritisation-shaped. Some dimensions ask you to re-score what you have already detected, this time on action urgency rather than evidence. Three dimensions ask something new that Part 1 does not.

The exercise is paper-and-pencil by design. It is meant to slow the conversation down. Boards under pressure tend to default to the language they already use about their organisation, and that is precisely what generic drift hides inside. The workbook asks you to put that language to one side for two and a half hours and look at the evidence honestly.

A note on scope. This workbook is a board-level diagnostic, not a management one. It is designed for directors, the Chair, and the CEO sitting together. Some of the questions will be uncomfortable. That is the work. If a question is uncomfortable to answer honestly, the honest answer is usually the one that matters.

---

## How to use this workbook

**Solo first, group second.** Each director should work through both parts on their own before the meeting. Bring your filled-in workbook to the board table. Compare and discuss your scores at the meeting. Differences in scores between directors are themselves diagnostic.

**Use the primary documents in Part 1.** Each signal in Part 1 includes a short list of things to check. Most of those things live inside your organisation's own primary documents, not in your memory of them. Read the documents. The point of the exercise is to look at the evidence rather than to recall an impression.

**Use the Part 1 evidence in Part 2.** Part 2 runs faster than Part 1 because the evidence work is already done. When a Part 2 dimension overlaps with a Part 1 signal, the cross-reference is named in the worksheet. Re-score for action, not for evidence.

**Be specific.** When the workbook asks "what evidence did you rely on," write a sentence or a citation, not a vibe. "The 2022 strategic plan, executive summary, paragraph 3" is a useful answer. "It feels like things have changed" is not.

**Rate honestly.** The five-point scale has anchors at 1 (no concern) and 5 (urgent). Most boards score between 2 and 4 on most signals and dimensions. A board that scores 1 everywhere has either not done the exercise carefully or is in a sector with such favourable conditions that the next downturn will surprise them.

**Two-or-more is the threshold.** Any single signal or dimension scoring at 4 or 5 deserves a board conversation. Any two scoring at 4 or 5 simultaneously deserves a board decision. The workbook is calibrated to surface that threshold honestly.

**Use the lined space.** Every worksheet has space for your evidence and for your reading. Use it. The act of writing the sentence is the diagnostic.

## Signal 1 — The verbal-actual lag

**Refresher.** The most reliable early signal of generic drift is the gap between the position the organisation remembers and the position it actually inhabits. The language stays stable. The operating reality changes underneath it. Marks and Spencer's chief-executive letters used the same competitive-positioning language across the twelve years their market share was eroding. Nokia's filings shifted from specific innovation-leadership language to generic connectivity language while smartphone share collapsed from forty-nine per cent to eight. The lag is not dishonesty. It is the inertia of a memory the leadership team still believes in.

**Detection sequence.** Take the last five to seven years of your organisation's primary documents. Look at:

1. The chief executive's letter in each year's annual report. Highlight every phrase that describes the organisation's competitive position. Compare the highlights side by side across the years.
2. The opening paragraph of each year's strategic plan. Where has the framing of "what makes this organisation distinctive" stayed identical?
3. The mission statement, vision statement, or values statement. When was it last revised? When was the operating reality last revised?
4. The headline numbers in each year's financial summary. Where has the trajectory changed in the same period the language has not?
5. Any single competitor whose market position has moved noticeably in the period. How is your own language describing where you sit relative to them? Has it caught up?

**Rate this signal.**

- [ ] **1.** No concern. Language is current, position is described honestly, the words have been revised when the reality changed.
- [ ] **2.** Minor lag. One or two phrases sound a year or two out of date. Easily refreshed.
- [ ] **3.** Visible gap. Several passages describe a competitive position the organisation arguably no longer holds. The board can name the gap.
- [ ] **4.** Material gap. The organisation's public language is describing a position the operating reality has clearly moved away from. Stakeholders may already have noticed.
- [ ] **5.** Urgent. Language and position have separated. Your own documents read as a description of the organisation you remember being.

## Signal 2 — Outsiders are seeing the problem first

**Refresher.** In every case in the research base for *Generic Drift*, external observers named the drift before internal documents acknowledged it. Analysts described Gap's brand-positioning problems from 2014. The company's strategic documents continued to describe the business in growth terms until approximately 2017. Academic brand researchers identified Qantas's weakening point of difference in 2014; the company's strategy documentation did not acknowledge brand-equity erosion until 2023. WeWork was identified as a real-estate company rather than a technology platform within hours of its S-1 filing. The implication is not flattering. If a respected outsider has been describing a problem consistently for years and the organisation still describes the position differently, the most useful working assumption is not that the outsider is wrong.

**Detection sequence.**

1. Gather two or three pieces of external commentary about your organisation from the last twelve months. Analysts. Trade press. Customer reviews. A peer's submission to a regulator that names you. Sector association commentary.
2. Highlight any consistent criticism, framing, or characterisation that appears in more than one source.
3. Now look for the same characterisation in your own most recent strategy document or annual report. Where is it acknowledged? Where is it absent?
4. Pay particular attention to customer feedback that uses warm but vague language. "They're solid, they're good people, they do much the same as the others." Warmth without specificity is often the customer-side expression of category-average drift.
5. Ask the directors: who outside this organisation has named something about our positioning in the last year that we have not seriously absorbed?

**Rate this signal.**

- [ ] **1.** No concern. External and internal characterisations of the organisation match.
- [ ] **2.** Minor mismatch. One outsider has named a small thing we have not yet addressed.
- [ ] **3.** Visible mismatch. Consistent external commentary describes us in terms our internal documents do not yet use.
- [ ] **4.** Material mismatch. Outside parties have been describing a problem for at least eighteen months that we still characterise as noise.
- [ ] **5.** Urgent. We are being described by serious external observers in terms that would be embarrassing to acknowledge inside the organisation, and that is precisely why we have not.

## Signal 3 — Benchmarking becomes defensive

**Refresher.** Benchmarking is not the problem. Used well, it is a serious governance tool. The problem is the moment benchmarking changes function. Instead of informing judgement, it starts protecting the organisation from having to make a more distinct choice. You can hear it in the language of meetings. "Everyone else is doing this." "We cannot be too far out of line with the sector." "That is not how the category talks." None of these sentences is foolish. Together they signal that peers are no longer comparators to think against; they are permission structures to defer to.

**Detection sequence.**

1. Recall the last three board strategic discussions. Count the number of times "what our peers are doing" was used as the primary reason for a recommendation.
2. Ask: in the last twelve months, has the board approved any strategic move that meaningfully differs from what the rest of the sector is doing? Name it.
3. Look at any benchmarking material the board has received recently. What is it being used for? Calibration ("are we operating efficiently"), or comfort ("we are within the band")? The two uses look identical and feel very different.
4. Try this question on the room: *what would we still do differently if no peer were doing it?* If the answer comes slowly, the drift is more advanced than anyone wishes.
5. Look at the most recent strategy paper presented to the board. Strike out every reference to peers. Does the remaining argument still hold up?

**Rate this signal.**

- [ ] **1.** No concern. Benchmarking informs but does not decide.
- [ ] **2.** Minor reliance. Peer practice has become a sense-check but not a substitute.
- [ ] **3.** Visible reliance. Several recent decisions defaulted to "this is what the sector does."
- [ ] **4.** Material reliance. Strategy conversations frequently end with "we cannot be too different from peers."
- [ ] **5.** Urgent. The board cannot name a strategic move it has approved that materially differs from sector norm.

## Signal 4 — Exclusion choices have become hard to name

**Refresher.** Healthy strategy creates not only emphasis but exclusion. It tells the organisation what not to pursue, what not to claim, what not to build, what trade-offs it accepts. One of the clearest warnings of generic drift is that leaders can still name admirable virtues but can no longer name credible exclusions. The strategy survives the meeting; it does not survive comparison with the actual exclusion choices the organisation has made.

**Detection sequence.**

1. Ask each director, on paper and individually, to name three things the organisation has deliberately decided not to pursue in the last two years. Three customers it has decided not to serve. Three capabilities it has decided not to build. Three markets it has decided not to enter.
2. Compare answers. Where directors give the same three exclusions, the strategy has real edges. Where directors give different three or struggle to name any, the strategy has been softened to accommodate everyone.
3. Look at the most recent strategic plan. Find the explicit "what we are not" passage. Is it specific, or does it consist of admirable virtues in the negative ("we will not compromise on quality", "we will not lose sight of values")?
4. Ask the CEO: in the last twelve months, name one opportunity the organisation declined for a reason other than capacity. If the answer is slow, the exclusion discipline is weaker than the strategy claims.
5. Look at the budget. Where has the spend shape changed materially in the last three years? Where has it stayed flat? Strategy is what gets resourced. Exclusion is what gets stopped.

**Rate this signal.**

- [ ] **1.** No concern. The board can quickly and consistently name what the organisation deliberately does not do.
- [ ] **2.** Minor blur. Directors name similar exclusions but with different specifics.
- [ ] **3.** Visible blur. Some exclusions are clear; others are vague or aspirational rather than acted on.
- [ ] **4.** Material blur. Directors give meaningfully different answers about what the organisation has deliberately declined.
- [ ] **5.** Urgent. The board cannot name a single concrete exclusion the organisation has made in the last two years.

## Signal 5 — Governance-form change is treated as merely structural

**Refresher.** Some of the fastest-onset generic drift in the case research came from organisations whose governance form changed without anyone treating that change as a positioning decision. Northern Rock's mutual-to-plc conversion removed the operational and cultural grounding of the mutual identity within a decade. The community-lender claim survived as marketing language for a few more years; the operating reality had already left the building. The same pattern applies to any organisation whose distinctiveness sits inside its governance: co-operatives, mutuals, professional bodies, not-for-profits, founder-led firms. A change to who the organisation answers to, how decisions are made, how capital is sourced, or what success is optimised for can quietly rewire the organisation's proposition long before the market language catches up.

**Detection sequence.**

1. In the last five years, has anything in the organisation's governance structure changed? Ownership, membership, capital, board composition, voting rights, founding leadership, parent or subsidiary relationships, regulatory category? Make the list.
2. For each item on the list, ask: which positioning claim depended on the prior structure? Has the claim been re-grounded in the new structure, or is it being asserted as if the change had not happened?
3. Look at the language the organisation still uses about itself, particularly any claims about community, mission, member-orientation, independence, or local roots. Was that claim true because of the old structure?
4. If a governance change is currently being considered, name explicitly which positioning claims will need to be revisited if the change goes through. If the board cannot name them, the change has not yet been treated as a positioning decision.
5. Read the most recent regulatory or annual filing. Where does the organisation's current legal description differ from the one used in its own marketing?

**Rate this signal.**

- [ ] **1.** No concern. No recent governance changes; or changes were openly treated as positioning decisions and the claims were revisited.
- [ ] **2.** Minor mismatch. A small governance change has not been fully threaded through positioning language.
- [ ] **3.** Visible mismatch. A material governance change has happened and some claims now sound aspirational rather than grounded.
- [ ] **4.** Material mismatch. The organisation is still claiming a position that the current governance form no longer supports.
- [ ] **5.** Urgent. The legal organisation and the rhetorical organisation are now visibly different things, and the board has not addressed the gap.

## Signal 6 — Leadership transition imports category logic faster than strategic memory

**Refresher.** One of the sharpest patterns in the case research is that drift often accelerates when leadership passes to people who were not formed inside the founding strategic culture. This is not because outsiders are inferior. It is the subtler problem that new leaders inherit a category before they inherit a memory. Unless the organisation has preserved its strategic logic with unusual discipline, fresh leaders will often import the norms of the wider field faster than they recover the reasons this organisation mattered in the first place. Marks and Spencer's senior buyer cohort retired through the late 1980s and 1990s, replaced by managers benchmarking against competitors rather than against the M&S sourcing standard. Gap could not stabilise a clear centre of gravity across four successive CEOs in twenty years. The mechanism is structural, not personal.

**Detection sequence.**

1. List the leadership transitions at executive and board level in the last five years. CEO, Chair, key executive roles, longstanding directors.
2. For each transition, ask: was the strategic logic the predecessor held actively transferred, or was it allowed to walk out the door with them? What document or briefing carried that logic forward?
3. Compare the strategy papers that arrived from new leaders within their first eighteen months with strategy papers from the prior period. Where has the language quietly become more category-standard?
4. Ask the longest-serving director: "Is there something we used to understand about this organisation that we no longer easily explain?" Listen carefully to the answer.
5. Look at the recruitment pipelines and senior succession discussions. Are candidates being assessed on their fit with this organisation's distinctiveness, or on their fit with the sector's standard executive profile?

**Rate this signal.**

- [ ] **1.** No concern. Transitions explicitly carry strategic memory; new leaders inherit it deliberately.
- [ ] **2.** Minor loss. One transition lost something subtle that has since been partly recovered.
- [ ] **3.** Visible loss. Some recent strategic decisions have a category-standard feel that the predecessor era did not have.
- [ ] **4.** Material loss. The organisation's distinctiveness is increasingly described as heritage rather than as live operating logic.
- [ ] **5.** Urgent. Veterans are saying things like "we used to know how to explain this." The strategic memory is leaving.

## Signal 7 — Scale starts breaking the thing the organisation says makes it special

**Refresher.** A consistent pattern in the case research: scale and standardisation often destroy community-embedded distinctiveness. WeWork's S-1 declared the company a "community company committed to maximum positive impact for our members" while disclosing a loss of five thousand dollars per member per year. The community premise required curation ratios and operational investments the scaling model could not sustain. The claim survived rhetorically for years after the operating reality had become indistinguishable from a serviced office. The same mechanism applies in healthcare, professional associations, educational institutions, faith-based and mission-led organisations, accreditation bodies, mutual insurers, and rural co-operatives. Every one of them routinely claims community, mission, or local embeddedness as a primary differentiator.

**Detection sequence.**

1. Name your organisation's single most distinctive claim in one sentence. Then name the operational practice that makes the claim real (the staffing ratio, the curation model, the local-knowledge requirement, the time investment, the named-relationship pattern).
2. Look at the trend in that operational practice over the last five years. Has it strengthened, held steady, or quietly thinned?
3. Look at the recent scaling decisions: new locations, new product lines, new client categories, new staff models, new technology platforms, new service tiers. For each one, ask whether the operational practice that grounds the distinctive claim was strengthened or compromised.
4. Read the most recent customer or member survey. Where do respondents use language consistent with the distinctive claim? Where do they use language any peer organisation could produce?
5. Compare the new-customer experience today with the experience five years ago. Where has the practice that grounded the claim been replaced by something more standardised?

**Rate this signal.**

- [ ] **1.** No concern. Scaling decisions have explicitly protected or strengthened the operational practice behind the distinctive claim.
- [ ] **2.** Minor thinning. One scaling move slightly weakened the practice but did not break it.
- [ ] **3.** Visible thinning. Several scaling moves have progressively standardised away the practice that made the claim real.

## Signal 8 — The organisation reaches for new language before rebuilding operations

**Refresher.** This is the failed recovery move. Organisations that sense drift often reach first for refreshed language. New campaigns. New taglines. New narratives. New declarations of intent. Sometimes this is necessary. Often it is premature. Qantas reactivated "Spirit of Australia" language in its 2023 recovery programme; the credibility question is whether the service operations now correspond to the brand claim. The partial counter-example in the case research is Marks and Spencer under Stuart Rose, where the sourcing model was rebuilt before stronger quality claims were leaned on again. The sequencing is everything. Operations first, language second is the only repair sequence that earns trust back. Language first widens the gap that already exists.

**Detection sequence.**

1. List the brand, messaging, positioning, narrative, or strategic-communications refresh projects underway or recently completed in your organisation.
2. For each one, ask whether the operating model behind the new language has been substantively rebuilt. Be honest. Marketing investment is not operational rebuild.
3. Look at staff sentiment. When new language arrives without operational change, employees know. Their conversations become more cynical, their public advocacy quieter.
4. Look at long-standing customers. Are they being asked to believe in something the actual service has not yet started doing?
5. Ask the CEO directly: in the last twelve months, where have we changed how we say things faster than we changed how we do things?

**Rate this signal.**

- [ ] **1.** No concern. Recent language change has been preceded by visible operational change.
- [ ] **2.** Minor mismatch. One narrative refresh runs ahead of the operating reality but is being followed up.
- [ ] **3.** Visible mismatch. Multiple narrative or brand projects are running ahead of operational change.
- [ ] **4.** Material mismatch. The organisation is actively asking stakeholders to believe claims the operating model does not yet support.
- [ ] **5.** Urgent. The trust gap is widening with every new campaign. Staff and customers can see it; leadership cannot.

## Reading the pattern

The total score is less interesting than the shape. A board that is healthy on six signals or dimensions and concerning on two is in a more defined position than a board that is mildly concerning on all sixteen. Use the questions below as the discussion frame.

**Which one or two items scored highest?** Those are the ones doing the most damage. They are also the ones with the clearest remediation path. Name them out loud. Write them down.

**Read Part 1 and Part 2 together where they overlap.** Where a Ch 6 sign and its matching Ch 10 dimension scored similarly, the evidence and the action question agree. Where they scored differently, the gap is informative. A sign scoring 4 (clear evidence of drift) but the matching dimension scoring 2 (low action urgency) tells you the board is seeing the drift but not yet treating it as urgent. The reverse tells you the board is anxious without yet having found the evidence.

**Pay particular attention to Dimensions 4, 5, and 6.** Those are the three lenses Part 2 adds that Part 1 does not provide. A high score on any of them is unusually significant because Part 1 will not have surfaced it. Hidden strengths ignored, customer relevance of claimed difference, and evidence of commoditisation each open a different conversation the rest of the workbook does not open.

**Which scores diverged between directors?** Where directors gave the same item a 2 and a 5, the disagreement itself is the diagnostic. Spend ten minutes on the gap. The director who scored higher is usually responding to something the director who scored lower has not yet absorbed.

**Are any two high-scoring items connected?** A high score on Signal 1 (verbal-actual lag) often comes with a high score on Signal 2 (outsiders see first); together they signal that the organisation has stopped looking at itself honestly. A high score on Dimension 5 (customer relevance) paired with a high score on Dimension 6 (commoditisation evidence) signals that the market is no longer crediting the organisation's positioning, which usually precedes pricing collapse. Look for the pairing. The pairing is the real story.

**What do the lowest scores tell you?** An item scoring at 1 or 2 is not just an absence of concern. It is also a strength. Name it. Boards often spend all their time on what is failing and forget to identify what they are doing well enough to protect.

## Recommended next moves

These are decisions a board can make in the meeting, not projects it commissions for next year. Pick the ones that match your scoring pattern.

**If Signal 1 (verbal-actual lag) or Dimension 1 (language sameness) scored 4 or 5**, the board can today commission a writing refresh. Not a brand campaign. A discipline: every annual report, every chair statement, every CEO letter from this year forward will name the one most important way the organisation's position has changed since the last one. The discipline takes ten minutes a year and saves the organisation from a decade of inertia.

**If Signal 2 (outsiders see first) scored 4 or 5**, the board can today commission a standing item on the board agenda titled "What outsiders are saying about us." Twenty minutes per meeting. The CEO presents two pieces of external commentary the organisation has not yet absorbed. The board decides which one is worth taking seriously.

**If Signal 3 (defensive benchmarking) or Dimension 2 (benchmark dependence) scored 4 or 5**, the board can today install a meeting rule: no recommendation passes without the room being able to answer the question, "what would we still do here if no peer were doing it?" The rule takes thirty seconds per agenda item and meaningfully changes what the executive team prepares.

**If Signal 4 (exclusion choices), Dimension 3 (risk aversion in choice), or Dimension 7 (sixty-second test) scored 4 or 5**, the board can today ask the CEO to bring a one-page "what we will deliberately not do" paper to the next strategy session. Three categorical exclusions. Specific. Resourced. Reviewable.

**If Signal 5 (governance change) scored 4 or 5**, the board can today resolve that any future governance change of any kind will be accompanied by a positioning-impact paper. The paper names which claims will need to be re-grounded if the change goes through. Most boards consider governance changes structurally; this resolution makes them consider them positionally as well.

**If Signal 6 (leadership transition) scored 4 or 5**, the board can today ask the Chair and CEO to produce a written "strategic memory" document within ninety days. What does this organisation understand about its own distinctiveness that the next leadership cohort will need to inherit? The document is short. It is also the most important thing on the desk.

**If Signal 7 (scale vs distinctiveness) scored 4 or 5**, the board can today amend the next scaling decision the executive brings forward. The amendment adds one paragraph: "How will this decision strengthen, hold, or weaken the operational practice that grounds our most distinctive claim?" The paragraph forces the question into the room.

**If Signal 8 (language before operations) scored 4 or 5**, the board can today pause any communications, brand, or messaging refresh project that is not paired with a named operational change. Hold the language until the practice is in place. The discipline is uncomfortable. It is also the only one of these recommendations that has saved any of the named cases in the book.

**If Dimension 4 (hidden strengths ignored) scored 4 or 5**, the board can today commission a one-page "operational distinctiveness audit." Not a marketing exercise. A list of things the organisation does materially better than its peers, with the evidence, brought back to the next meeting. The audit alone often surfaces three claims the strategy has been missing.

**If Dimension 5 (customer relevance of claimed difference) scored 4 or 5**, the board can today commission a six-customer conversation programme. Six unprompted phone calls or coffees with current customers in the next ninety days. The single question: "In your own words, why do you choose to work with us?" The answers are the test of whether the strategy's claimed differentiator is felt by the people it is meant to influence.

**If Dimension 6 (evidence of commoditisation) scored 4 or 5**, the board can today add a standing line to every board pack: pricing pressure metrics, discount frequency, premium-tier health. Three numbers. Tracked quarter on quarter. The numbers are the leading commercial indicators of generic drift; once they are on the agenda, the conversation about them follows.

**If Dimension 8 (execution rhythm) scored 4 or 5**, the board can today commission a cadence review: three months from now, the Chair brings back a one-page review of the operational rhythm the organisation actually runs on, the rhythm it should be running on, and the gap between them. The review is the natural bridge into the first volume of the trilogy, *Cadence Drift and The Rhythm Advantage*.

## What this workbook does not tell you

Three things this workbook is not, so the board does not over-trust it.

It is not a diagnostic by itself. Eight directors filling in this workbook is the start of a board conversation, not its conclusion. The signals in Part 1 and the dimensions in Part 2 are calibrated to surface real conditions. The interpretation, the decisions, and the changes are the board's work.

It is not a substitute for the book. The book carries the case research, the academic evidence, the named-case histories that show how generic drift unfolds over years and decades. Chapter 6 carries the eight warning signs in their full argument. Chapter 10 carries the eight dimensions as the diagnostic in its full form. The workbook applies both lenses. The lenses are sharper if you have read the chapters first.

It is not the only way to look at your organisation. Generic drift is one form of strategic drift. Rhythm drift (the loss of cadence at which strategy is tested against evidence) is another. Sovereignty drift (the loss of control over IP, AI use, and distributed work) is a third. The three together form the Carlorbiz Drift Trilogy, and the same board can productively run all three diagnostics across a year. This one is the one for this book.

Most board exercises end the way most strategy days end: with a document that no one returns to. The instrument was never the problem. The problem is that audits, like plans, decay without a place to live.

The sixteen ratings you have just produced do not need software to be useful. They need an honest hour, the right people in the room, and the willingness to write down what you actually saw. You have just done that.

If, after that hour, your organisation decides the diagnostic is worth running as an operating discipline rather than as a one-off board exercise, a working installation is available at **carlorbiz.com.au/rhythm**.
